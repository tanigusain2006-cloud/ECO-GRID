Understanding Waste

Every item we discard follows a path ‚Äî through collection systems, processing facilities, and ultimately back into the environment or the economy. How well waste is sorted at the source determines whether materials are recovered and reused, or whether they contribute to pollution, greenhouse gas emissions, and resource depletion. Understanding where things go is the first step toward making choices that actually matter.

Organic

What happens to a banana peel in a compost facility?

When organic waste reaches a composting facility, microorganisms break it down into humus ‚Äî a stable, nutrient-dense material that improves soil structure and water retention. The process takes 4 to 8 weeks under managed conditions, far faster than decomposition in a sealed landfill. The end product is used in agriculture and urban landscaping to reduce dependency on synthetic fertilizers.

Did you know? Composting food scraps returns carbon to the soil, where it benefits plant growth ‚Äî instead of releasing it as CO‚ÇÇ or methane into the atmosphere.
Organic

Why does buried food waste produce methane?

In a sealed landfill, food waste decomposes without oxygen ‚Äî a process called anaerobic decomposition. Unlike composting, which produces CO‚ÇÇ, anaerobic decay generates methane (CH‚ÇÑ), a greenhouse gas that traps heat roughly 80 times more effectively than CO‚ÇÇ over a 20-year period. Landfills are one of the largest human-made sources of methane emissions globally.

Did you know? Some landfills capture methane and convert it to electricity ‚Äî but this only works at scale. Diverting food waste to composting eliminates the methane problem at the source.
Organic

How does composting regenerate soil?

Healthy soil contains billions of microorganisms per gram. Industrial agriculture depletes these communities over time through tilling and synthetic inputs. Compost reintroduces organic matter, creating conditions for fungal networks and bacteria that help plants absorb nutrients and water. Studies show that fields amended with compost consistently outperform those using synthetic fertilizer alone over multi-year periods.

Did you know? A single teaspoon of healthy compost-amended soil can contain more microorganisms than there are humans on Earth.
Organic

How much food waste is produced globally?

The Food and Agriculture Organization estimates that approximately one-third of all food produced for human consumption ‚Äî around 1.3 billion tonnes ‚Äî is lost or wasted every year. If food waste were a country, it would be the third-largest emitter of greenhouse gases in the world. The majority occurs in developed countries at the consumer stage, meaning sorting and composting decisions made at home genuinely matter.

Did you know? The resources used to grow wasted food ‚Äî water, land, energy, and labor ‚Äî are also wasted. Reducing food waste is considered one of the highest-impact climate actions available to individuals.
Recyclable

The lifecycle of a plastic bottle

A plastic bottle recycled correctly is shredded into flakes, cleaned, and melted into pellets used to manufacture new bottles, polyester fiber, or packaging. Without recycling, plastic persists in the environment for 400‚Äì500 years, breaking into microplastics that enter waterways and food chains. Only about 9% of all plastic ever produced has been recycled; the rest has been landfilled, incinerated, or released into the environment.

Did you know? Making a new plastic bottle from recycled material uses roughly 75% less energy than producing one from virgin petroleum feedstock.
Recyclable

Why can glass be recycled infinitely?

Glass is made from silica sand, soda ash, and limestone ‚Äî materials that can be melted and reformed repeatedly without losing purity or structural integrity. Unlike plastic, which degrades chemically with each recycling cycle, glass remains identical in quality whether it has been recycled once or a hundred times. Recycling glass also reduces the temperature required for production, lowering energy use and carbon emissions.

Did you know? Recycling one glass bottle saves enough energy to power a 100-watt light bulb for about four hours. The UK recycles over 70% of its glass packaging annually.
Recyclable

How is paper actually recycled?

Collected paper is mixed with water in a pulper to create a slurry of fibers. Contaminants like ink and staples are removed through flotation and screening. The clean pulp is then pressed, dried, and rolled into new paper products. Each recycling cycle shortens the cellulose fibers slightly, which is why recycled paper is eventually mixed with some virgin fiber. Still, paper recycling uses roughly 60% less energy than virgin production and saves significant quantities of water and trees.

Did you know? Recycling one tonne of paper saves approximately 17 trees, 7,000 gallons of water, and 380 gallons of oil compared to making paper from raw timber.
Recyclable

Aluminium: the energy story of recycling

Extracting aluminium from bauxite ore is enormously energy intensive ‚Äî it requires mining, refining into alumina, and then electrolytic smelting at very high temperatures. Recycling aluminium bypasses nearly the entire process. It takes only about 5% of the energy needed to produce new aluminium from raw ore. This makes aluminium one of the most economically and environmentally valuable materials to recycle, and it can be recycled indefinitely without quality loss.

Did you know? The aluminium in a recycled can could be back on a store shelf as a new can within as little as 60 days of being collected.
Hazardous

How do batteries contaminate soil and water?

Household batteries contain heavy metals including lead, cadmium, mercury, and lithium. When disposed of in regular landfill, the battery casing corrodes over years and these metals leach into surrounding soil and groundwater. Cadmium, for example, is classified as a Group 1 carcinogen and accumulates in the kidneys. A single AA battery can contaminate approximately 400 liters of water. Specialist battery recycling facilities recover these metals safely, preventing environmental release.

Did you know? In the EU, battery collection and recycling is legally mandated under the Battery Directive. Many supermarkets and electronics stores provide free battery drop-off points.
Hazardous

What is chemical leaching, and why does it matter?

Chemical leaching occurs when rain water passes through landfill material and picks up dissolved or suspended chemicals, forming a liquid called leachate. This can carry solvents, pesticides, heavy metals, and other toxins into groundwater used for drinking. Modern lined landfills attempt to capture leachate, but older sites and illegal dumps often have no containment. Keeping household chemicals ‚Äî bleach, paint, solvents ‚Äî out of general waste dramatically reduces the risk of leachate contamination.

Did you know? Some chemical contaminants from landfill leachate are detectable in groundwater samples decades after a site has been closed and capped.
Hazardous

Medical waste and why it needs special handling

Sharps, expired medications, and contaminated dressings can harbor pathogens capable of transmitting infections if not properly contained. Medical waste incineration at temperatures above 1000¬∞C destroys pathogens and breaks down most toxic compounds. Medications washed down drains can pass through water treatment systems and enter rivers, where they affect the endocrine systems of aquatic life. Many pharmacies accept unused medications for safe disposal.

Did you know? Scientists have detected synthetic estrogen from discarded hormonal medications in river water across Europe and North America, affecting reproductive systems in fish populations.
Hazardous

Why is paint disposal so important?

Oil-based paints contain volatile organic compounds (VOCs) and solvents that evaporate and contribute to air pollution, or leach into soil if poured down drains or discarded in landfill. Water-based (latex) paints are less toxic but still contain biocides and pigments that should not enter waterways. Many municipalities run hazardous household waste collection days where paint is accepted. Leftover paint can also be donated to community projects, extending its useful life entirely.

Did you know? In the UK, over 50 million liters of usable paint are thrown away each year. Community reuse schemes redistribute a significant portion of this to schools, charities, and social housing.
E-Waste

Rare earth mining and the cost of electronics

Modern electronics depend on rare earth elements ‚Äî neodymium in speakers, indium in touchscreens, cobalt in batteries. Extracting these materials requires open-pit mining that disrupts ecosystems and produces radioactive tailings. Many rare earth deposits are concentrated in environmentally sensitive regions. Recovering these elements from e-waste through urban mining requires far less energy and has a fraction of the environmental impact of primary extraction.

Did you know? One tonne of mobile phones contains more gold than one tonne of gold ore. Urban mining of e-waste is economically competitive with primary metal extraction for many precious metals.
E-Waste

E-waste in developing countries

An estimated 50 million tonnes of e-waste are generated globally each year, and a significant portion is exported ‚Äî often illegally ‚Äî to processing sites in West Africa and Southeast Asia. Workers at informal sites burn circuit boards to recover metals, inhaling toxic fumes containing dioxins, lead, and brominated flame retardants. Children in these communities show elevated blood lead levels and cognitive effects. Responsible e-waste recycling keeps these materials within regulated, protective systems.

Did you know? The Basel Convention prohibits the export of hazardous waste from developed to developing nations, but enforcement remains inconsistent and e-waste often moves under false documentation.
E-Waste

What toxins are in a circuit board?

Printed circuit boards are laminated with brominated flame retardants (BFRs) to reduce fire risk. When incinerated improperly, BFRs release polybrominated dibenzo-p-dioxins (PBDDs) ‚Äî persistent organic pollutants that bioaccumulate in fatty tissue. Circuit boards also contain lead solder, cadmium in older components, and hexavalent chromium in surface finishes. Certified e-waste recyclers use mechanical shredding and controlled smelting to recover metals without generating these byproducts.

Did you know? The European Union's RoHS Directive (Restriction of Hazardous Substances) has largely eliminated lead and cadmium from new consumer electronics since 2006, making newer devices safer to recycle.
E-Waste

How phone recycling programs actually work

Manufacturer take-back programs and certified recyclers accept old phones for disassembly. The device is first wiped of data, then separated into components: the screen assembly, battery, circuit board, and housing. Each stream goes to specialized processors ‚Äî batteries are handled separately due to lithium fire risk, screens are shredded and their indium recovered, and circuit boards are smelted in high-temperature furnaces that capture metal fumes. The recovered materials re-enter supply chains.

Did you know? Apple's Daisy robot can disassemble 1.2 million iPhone units per year, recovering cobalt, lithium, and rare earth magnets that re-enter Apple's own supply chain ‚Äî reducing the need for new mining.

The Circular Economy

A circular economy is an economic system designed to eliminate waste and keep materials in use for as long as possible. Unlike the traditional linear model ‚Äî extract, produce, use, discard ‚Äî a circular system treats the end of one product's life as the beginning of another's. Materials flow through the economy in continuous loops, reducing the need for virgin resource extraction and lowering the total environmental impact of consumption.

Waste sorting is the essential gateway into circular systems. Materials that are not correctly separated at the source cannot enter these recovery loops. A contaminated batch of recyclables may be entirely landfilled. An unsorted e-waste device loses all its recoverable metals. The decisions made in the moment of disposal ‚Äî which bin, which facility, which program ‚Äî directly determine whether a material enters a new cycle or becomes environmental burden.
